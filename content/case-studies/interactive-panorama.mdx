## Problem

AEO's editorial team wanted to ship an immersive product guide — something that broke out of
the standard category-page grid and let customers explore a styled environment from inside it.
The challenge: in 2014, browser-native 360° viewers didn't exist. WebGL was experimental. The
options were Flash, custom JavaScript with severe browser limitations, or a third-party
embedded player.

## Approach

I leveraged the **PanoVR (Pano2VR) framework** to build a shoppable interactive panoramic
product guide. The panorama itself was a stitched 360° photograph; on top of it I layered
clickable hotspots that linked to product detail pages. The result felt like walking through a
curated retail scene — except every visible product was buyable.

The hotspot data was modeled as a separate JSON config so the editorial team could re-skin
the experience for future campaigns without touching the player.

## Outcome

- Shipped one of AEO's earliest immersive editorial experiences
- Established a reusable pattern for hotspot-driven shoppable content

## Technical highlights

- **Pano2VR** as the rendering engine (desktop-targeted; mobile fallback was a flat image)
- **JSON-driven hotspot config** for editorial reuse across campaigns
- **XML scene configuration** generated from the design source files

## Demo status

The original implementation depends on the Pano2VR Flash/SWF runtime and is no longer
functional in modern browsers. The case study preserves the work as screenshots and recorded
walkthrough video.

## What I led, not just shipped

A reminder that "novel" experiences age fast. The work was state-of-the-art when shipped and
unusable a decade later. The lesson stuck: when building for a specific runtime, separate
*content* from *renderer* so the content survives even if the renderer doesn't.
