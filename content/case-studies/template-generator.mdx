## Problem

At AEO, every new customer-facing content module — a marketing card, a promo tile, a campaign
landing block — required a **three-month engineering process**: design → spec → engineering
build → QA → release. By the time a module shipped, the campaign it was built for had often
moved on. The design team wanted control; the engineering team didn't want to keep building
one-off variants of the same five layouts.

## Approach

I built an integrated **JavaScript-based templating interface** that handed module creation
back to the design team. Designers picked from a library of pre-built layouts, configured
the content slots, and the module rendered identically to the engineered version — no release
required.

The technical bet: most "new modules" weren't really new. They were re-skins of a small set
of layouts with different content, imagery, and copy. By formalizing the layouts as templates
and giving designers a config interface, the customer-facing variety stayed high while the
engineering work dropped to near zero.

## Outcome

- Eliminated the **3-month development cycle** for customer-facing content modules
- Design team gained direct control over module deployment and iteration
- Increased flexibility — designers experimented with module variants without engineering
  involvement
- Engineering team freed up for higher-leverage work

## Technical highlights

- A **template library** of pre-built, design-approved module layouts
- A **configuration interface** that let designers fill template slots and preview the
  rendered output
- Integration with the headless CMS so configured modules were treated as first-class content
  entries

## What I led, not just shipped

The hardest part wasn't the templating engine — it was convincing engineering leadership that
*relinquishing control* of module creation was the right move. Once the design team had the
tool, the cycle time drop was self-evident.
